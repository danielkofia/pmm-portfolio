Part 01 · Problem Discovery

Starting with an observed problem, not a product idea

The strongest PMM thinking begins before any product exists — by observing real behavior, identifying a pattern, and asking what system failure is causing it.

A patient on long-term hypertension medication kept returning to the counter — not for his monthly refill, but to beg for two or three pills because he had run out. The staff were frustrated. The patient was embarrassed. The pharmacy lost the full-course sale. This pattern repeated across dozens of patients and pharmacies. It was not a character flaw. It was a system gap.

The Core Market Observation

Every health-tech company active in Ghana — mPharma, Redbird, Bisa, Talamus — is addressing the access problem: getting medicine, doctors, or diagnostics to patients. The post-encounter space — what happens after a patient leaves the pharmacy — is not yet a defined product category in this market. Retenza was designed to explore whether that gap represents a viable product opportunity.

Part 02 · Customer Research & Discovery

Applying The Mom Test before building anything

The rule: never ask anyone if your idea is good. Ask about their life, their current behavior, and what it costs them. People are polite — they'll say "great idea" to almost anything. What actually matters is whether they're already spending time or money trying to solve the problem you want to fix.

How the research was structured

01
Identify the right conversation partners
Three tiers were targeted deliberately: pharmacy owners (the paying customer), pharmacists (clinical authority and internal champions), and pharmacy technicians & assistants (the most candid source of counter-level reality — peer conversations reduce hierarchy-driven filtering).
02
Lead with behavioral questions, not idea validation
Every conversation was structured around what they are already doing, what it costs them, and whether they experience the problem emotionally. Asking "Would you buy this?" was explicitly avoided — it produces social affirmation, not signal.
03
Use responses to segment, not just to validate
The discovery conversations weren't just about confirming the problem existed. They were used to identify which pharmacy owners would be early adopters vs. which ones weren't yet experiencing the problem as painful enough to act.
04
Note what wasn't said as carefully as what was
Most pharmacy owners had never connected patient dropout to a revenue number. This absence of awareness was itself a product-relevant finding: the category would need to be introduced before the solution could be sold.

The five discovery questions

Each question was designed to surface a specific insight without leading the respondent.

Q1
How often do patients on long-term medication actually come back consistently?
Reveals whether they even recognize the retention problem exists.
Q2
What happens to your business when a chronic patient doesn't return for their refill?
Prompts them to calculate the revenue impact, often for the first time.
Q3
Have you ever tried anything to follow up with patients after they leave?
Exposes existing workarounds — the real current "competitor."
Q4
What does it cost you — in time or stress — to deal with patients who come in asking for two or three pills?
Quantifies the operational disruption that isn't visible in revenue figures alone.
Q5
If you could fix one thing about how your patients behave, what would it be?
Surfaces the problem they care about most without the researcher naming it first.

Customer segmentation from discovery patterns

Responses consistently grouped pharmacy owners into two behavioral profiles. Identifying these before investing further time was a key prioritization output of the research process.

Early Adopter Profile — Prioritize
  • ✓ Gives specific examples of lost patients without being prompted
  • ✓ Shows visible frustration when the topic of dropout comes up
  • ✓ Has already attempted some informal follow-up via calls or WhatsApp
  • ✓ Calculates or estimates revenue loss when prompted
  • ✓ Describes patient consistency as the change they most want
Not Yet Ready — Deprioritize
  • ✗ Gives vague answers; no specific examples of dropout or lost revenue
  • ✗ Accepts patient dropout as normal — shows no urgency around it
  • ✗ Has never tried any form of patient follow-up
  • ✗ Relies entirely on location, walk-ins, and word of mouth
  • ✗ Shows no emotional energy when discussing the problem
Key Research Finding

Most pharmacy owners surveyed had never calculated the revenue impact of patient dropout. This isn't evidence that the market doesn't exist — it's evidence that the problem hasn't been given a price tag yet. The implication for GTM: the first sales conversation isn't a product demo. It's a revenue conversation that makes an invisible cost visible.

This finding is observational and directional. Formal validation would require structured interviews with a larger, more representative sample.

Signal strength: Very strong · Heard in: 5/10 conversations
Theme 03 · Counter Disruption
"Begging patient" scenarios cause visible staff frustration

Patients returning for 2–3 pills mid-cycle are emotionally disruptive — staff feel disrespected, patients feel embarrassed. This is a hidden cost separate from lost revenue. It surfaced in almost every conversation unprompted, which means it's a behavioral pain point, not just an economic one.

Signal strength: Strong · Heard in: 9/10 conversations
Theme 04 · Trust Dynamics
Patients trust pharmacy staff more than hospital systems

A consistent observation across years at the counter: patients disclose more to pharmacy staff than to clinical doctors — partly accessibility, partly familiarity. A WhatsApp message from "your pharmacy" carries a different trust weight than one from a health app or hospital number. This informs message tone decisions.

Signal strength: Directional · Source: Counter observation
Theme 05 · Objection Pattern
Type Two owners cite "not enough time" — but the real barrier is low urgency

When owners said "we don't have time for this," probe questions revealed they spend more time managing the disruption of non-adherent patients than a follow-up system would cost. The time objection is a proxy for low perceived urgency — a category education problem, not a product problem.

Signal strength: Consistent · GTM implication: Sell urgency first
Theme 06 · Channel Fit
WhatsApp is the de facto communication layer for everything

Pharmacy owners use WhatsApp to manage suppliers, staff, and occasional patients. Patients use it for everything. No behavioral shift is required — the channel is already embedded in daily routines for both sides. An app would face adoption resistance. A WhatsApp message won't.

Signal strength: Very strong · No counter-evidence observed

Part 04 · Positioning

Building a positioning brief before any messaging work begins

Positioning answers three questions: who is this for, what does it do for them, and why should they believe us over anyone else. I worked through all three before writing a single word of messaging.

One-Line Position
"The pharmacy doesn't end at the door."

Retenza helps community pharmacies retain chronic patients after they leave. Most pharmacies lose patients silently because there's no follow-up system after dispensing. We use WhatsApp to automate refill reminders, adherence check-ins, and patient engagement — so pharmacies recover lost revenue while actually improving patient health outcomes.

Target audience framework

Primary — Paying Customer
Pharmacy Owner / Manager
Independent community pharmacy owners in Ghana's urban and peri-urban areas. Manage 50–300 patients monthly. Run lean operations with no structured post-dispensing follow-up. Business-minded but time-constrained.
Secondary — End User
Chronic Disease Patient
Low-to-middle income Ghanaian adults managing recurring conditions — hypertension, diabetes, malaria. Active daily WhatsApp users. High trust in local pharmacies. Miss medication not from carelessness but from lack of structured support.
Future — Potential Partner
Pharma & Health Insurance
Pharmaceutical manufacturers and health insurers with direct financial incentives to improve adherence outcomes. A hypothetical longer-term monetization angle, not a near-term GTM focus.

Why this positioning is credible — four differentiation pillars

Built from the inside
I didn't research this market from a desk. I worked in it for 5 years. I know how pharmacy owners think, what frustrates pharmacists, and how patients actually behave after they leave the counter. That context shapes every decision in this product.
WhatsApp — not an app
Patients in Ghana already use WhatsApp every day. Asking them to download something new is asking them to do extra work. Retenza meets them where they already are. No app, no data bundle, no learning curve.
Fits next to existing players
mPharma and Redbird handle access — getting medicine and diagnostics to patients. Retenza handles what comes after. We're not trying to replace them. A partnership pitch is more credible than a competitive one, especially early.
Domain knowledge as advantage
Most health-tech founders in this space are solving a problem they read about. I'm solving one I watched happen every day for five years. That's a different level of understanding — and it shows in the product decisions.
The market is already doing this
A pharmacy chain operator managing 10+ locations told me his team already follows up with patients via WhatsApp groups. Manually, with no tracking. Retenza doesn't need to convince the market the behavior is worth doing. It just needs to make it work properly.

Part 05 · Messaging Framework

Translating positioning into words that actually land

Pharmacy owners and patients need completely different messages. Different problems, different language, different things they care about. I built a framework for each — so the same product is communicated in two entirely different ways depending on who's reading.

To pharmacy owners — lead with revenue, not technology

Pharmacy owners are business operators first. They respond to revenue impact, operational simplicity, and competitive differentiation — in that order. Technology is a secondary concern.

Message Pillar Core Message Rationale
Revenue Recovery Based on estimated dropout patterns, you may be losing GHS 10–15K per month in silent refill revenue. Retenza is designed to recover it. Frames the problem as a business cost before introducing any solution. Needs validation with actual pharmacy data.
Zero Added Workload Your staff does nothing extra. Patient follow-up runs automatically via WhatsApp. You see outcomes on a dashboard. Addresses the most common objection before it's raised: "We don't have time for this."
Differentiation A patient who feels followed up with chooses you over the closer pharmacy. That loyalty isn't built on price — it's built on care. Speaks to the pharmacy owner's longer-term competitive concern beyond immediate revenue.
Care at Scale One pharmacist cannot personally follow up 200 patients. Retenza scales what your best staff member would do if they had the time. Bridges clinical values and business efficiency — relevant to both pharmacists and owners.

The revenue figures in the messaging would need to be validated and adjusted per pharmacy before use in actual sales conversations.

To patients — three principles governing message tone

Principle 01
Human before clinical

Patients respond to feeling recognized, not managed. Every message uses their name, acknowledges their daily reality, and avoids clinical or lecturing language.

Principle 02
One idea per message

No message contains more than one piece of information. Cognitive overload works against adherence. Short, clear, one action or response requested.

Principle 03
Two-way by design

Every check-in invites a response. Research indicates two-way messaging outperforms one-way reminders for adherence outcomes. The conversation is part of the product.

Part 06 · Go-To-Market Plan

A staged GTM approach — concierge first, scale later

Retenza isn't walking into a market with established buyers. It's introducing a new type of solution to people who don't yet have a name for the problem. That changes how you go to market. You don't launch — you prove, then you scale.

PHASE 01
Concierge MVP
Months 1–2 · 3–5 pharmacies · Manual operation
Enroll 3–5 Type One pharmacy owners identified in discovery. Run the service manually — messages sent by hand, outcomes tracked in a spreadsheet. The goal isn't efficiency; it's learning. Does the patient messaging model work? Do pharmacy owners see value? What breaks? Objective: First proof of engagement before any automation investment.
PHASE 02
Paid Pilot
Months 3–4 · 2–3 pharmacies · GHS 150/month intro pricing
Move the 1–2 most engaged Phase 1 pharmacies to a paid arrangement at a low introductory rate. Connect WhatsApp Business API to partially automate message sending. Produce a simple outcome report showing response rates and refill patterns. Objective: First revenue, first case study, first testimonial — the building blocks for sales at scale.
PHASE 03
Controlled Expansion
Months 5–8 · 10–15 pharmacies · Accra and Kumasi
Use the Phase 2 case study as the primary sales asset. Introduce tiered pricing. Add a part-time customer success resource. Begin partnership conversations with mPharma and Redbird as distribution channels. Objective: 10 paying pharmacies, 200+ enrolled patients, and enough data to refine the product-market fit thesis.
PHASE 04
West Africa Expansion Hypothesis
Months 9–18 · Nigeria and Ivory Coast · Partnership-led entry
If the Ghana model produces repeatable unit economics, explore market replication using the same playbook with local distribution partners. Begin institutional partnership conversations with pharma and insurance players using accumulated adherence data. This phase depends entirely on Phase 3 outcomes — it's a directional hypothesis, not a committed plan.

Case Study 02 · Independent PMM Audit

MEDvidi — Positioning & Messaging Strategy

I landed on MEDvidi's homepage and something felt immediately off. It was generic — the kind of messaging that sounds like it was written for everybody, which means it really speaks to nobody. When you're building something that genuinely helps people, that's a problem. Not just a revenue problem — a people problem. The right message finds the right person, speaks directly to their pain, and wraps the solution in an outcome that makes them feel like it was built specifically for them. That's what was missing here. And that's what I set out to fix.

What's wrong with this — and why it matters

Read that headline again. "Your mental wellness is one click away." Could BetterHelp say that? Could Talkspace say that? Could a meditation app say that? Yes to all three. When your headline could belong to any competitor, it effectively belongs to none of you. The person who really needs MEDvidi — who has already tried therapy apps, already waited weeks for a psychiatrist, and just needs someone to diagnose them and give them a prescription fast — lands on this page and has no idea that MEDvidi is exactly what they've been looking for. The best product in the room shouldn't be the quietest voice in it. That's what this audit is about fixing.

Section 1 · Business Impact — Why This Matters

Weak messaging isn't just a creative problem. It's a revenue problem.

The Real Problem

When someone lands on MEDvidi's homepage and can't tell in five seconds why it's different from BetterHelp, they leave. Not because the product is bad — because the message didn't hold them. That's not a traffic problem. That's a positioning problem. And positioning problems are fixable.

The Revenue Upside

Even a 5% improvement in homepage conversion means thousands more in monthly revenue — without spending a single extra dollar on ads. Better messaging doesn't just improve marketing. It improves every touchpoint: the homepage, the emails, the ads, the sales conversations. Fix the message, and everything downstream gets better.

MEDvidi's Real Price

$195 initial visit. $159 follow-up. A patient who stays for three months is worth $513 in revenue. Losing that patient at the homepage headline — because the message didn't speak to them — is losing $513 you already had a product good enough to earn.

This isn't about making the website prettier. It's about making sure the right person — the one who actually needs what MEDvidi offers — recognises themselves in the message and takes the next step.

Section 3 · Pain Points

The primary pain isn't the condition. It's the broken system.

This is the insight that changes everything about how MEDvidi should position itself. Customers aren't just looking for mental health care. They're looking for mental health care that fits their life after the system has already failed them.

⏱
Long waitlists

Traditional therapy and psychiatry practices often require 3–6 week waits. Alex doesn't have 3–6 weeks. Alex has Monday morning.

🧾
Insurance complexity

45 minutes on hold before care even begins. MEDvidi charges per visit, no insurance needed. But this advantage is never communicated on the homepage.

💊
Therapy-only platforms

BetterHelp and Talkspace offer therapy. No diagnosis. No medication path. For Alex who needs a prescription, these platforms are a dead end.

📉
Worsening performance

Symptoms reduce productivity, focus, and professional performance. Every week without treatment is another week running at 60%.

🤫
Shame and stigma

Fear of judgement makes seeking in-person care uncomfortable. A discreet, online-first solution removes this barrier — but MEDvidi doesn't lead with it.

Section 5 · Signals of Market Change

Why the timing for sharper positioning is right now

A PMM doesn't just look at where the market is — they look at where it's going. These four signals all point in the same direction: the window for MEDvidi to own a distinctive position is open, but it won't stay open.

⚖️
DEA Regulation Tightening

Rules around online prescribing of controlled substances are extended through December 2026 — but are under review. Platforms that demonstrate clinical rigor now will be better positioned if regulation tightens. MEDvidi's clinical model is a strength. It needs to say so louder.

Urgency signal — act before the window tightens
🤖
AI Entering Mental Health

AI symptom checkers, AI therapy bots, and diagnostic tools are growing fast. This creates both opportunity and competitive pressure. MEDvidi's human clinical model is a differentiator — but only if the messaging makes "real licensed clinician" a feature, not a footnote.

Competitive pressure — position human care as the premium
📈
ADHD & Anxiety Destigmatisation

Growing professional awareness of ADHD and anxiety is driving demand for diagnosis and treatment among previously untapped segments. The ICP is actively expanding. MEDvidi needs to be visible and specific enough to catch this wave.

Demand signal — the market is coming to them
🏢
Employer Mental Health Benefits

Employer mental health benefits are expanding rapidly — creating a B2B channel where MEDvidi could partner with companies as a mental health benefit provider. This is a future GTM opportunity that sharper positioning today would unlock.

Growth signal — B2B channel becoming viable

You check your insurance and spend 45 minutes on hold. You consider taking a half day off work just to sit in a waiting room for an appointment that lasts 20 minutes. So you close the tab. You go back to work. And the cycle continues.

Diagnosis. Treatment. Medication. One place. Fast.

Because mental health care shouldn't require you to put your life on hold to get better. It should fit into your life. It should move at the speed your life demands.

You've already waited long enough. You've already lost enough productive mornings, enough focused hours, enough good days to something that is treatable. If you've had that tab open and closed it more times than you can count — this is your sign. Getting better doesn't have to be complicated. It just has to start.
